TITLE: The Strong and the Weak
WORK: Twilight of the Idols
SECTION: Skirmishes in a War with the Age, selected excerpt
AUTHOR: Friedrich Nietzsche
THEMES: strength, weakness, egoism, hierarchy, decadence
SOURCE_TYPE: text

The selfless morality, a morality under which selfishness withers, is in all circumstances a bad sign.

The best are lacking when selfishness begins to be lacking.

If a man represent the ascending line of life, his value is of course extraordinary—and for the sake of the collective life which in him makes one step forward, the concern about his maintenance may even be extreme.

If he represent declining development, decay, chronic degeneration, sickness, he is of little worth, and the purest equity would have him take away as little as possible from those who are lucky strokes of nature.

Selfishness has as much value as the physiological value of him who practises it: its worth may be great, or it may be worthless and contemptible.

The weak have more intellect. In order to acquire intellect, one must be in need of it. One loses it when one no longer needs it.

Species do not evolve towards perfection: the weak always prevail over the strong—simply because they are the majority, and because they are also the more crafty.
