TITLE: The Criminal and Greatness Under Pressure
WORK: Twilight of the Idols
SECTION: Skirmishes in a War with the Age, selected excerpt
AUTHOR: Friedrich Nietzsche
THEMES: criminality, pressure, greatness, genius, society
SOURCE_TYPE: text

The criminal type is the type of the strong man amid unfavourable conditions, a strong man made sick.

He lacks the wild and savage state, a form of nature and existence which is freer and more dangerous, in which everything that constitutes the shield and the sword in the instinct of the strong man, takes a place by right.

Society puts a ban upon his virtues; the most spirited instincts inherent in him immediately become involved with the depressing passions, with suspicion, fear and dishonour.

When a man has to do that which he is best suited to do, which he is most fond of doing, not only clandestinely, but also with long suspense, caution and ruse, he becomes anæmic.

The criminal and the pioneer of the spirit can begin to resemble one another under hostile conditions.

Great men, like great ages, are explosive material, in which a stupendous amount of power is accumulated.

The first conditions of their existence are always historical and physiological; they are the outcome of the fact that for long ages energy has been collected, hoarded up, saved up and preserved for their use.

When the tension in the bulk has become sufficiently excessive, the most fortuitous stimulus suffices in order to call “genius,” “great deeds,” and momentous fate into the world.

Great men are necessary, the age in which they appear is a matter of chance; the fact that they almost invariably master their age is accounted for simply by the fact that they are stronger, that they are older, and that power has been stored longer for them.

The genius—in work and in deed—is necessarily a squanderer: the fact that he spends himself constitutes his greatness.
